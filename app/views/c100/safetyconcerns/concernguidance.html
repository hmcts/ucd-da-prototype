Safety concerns

The court needs to know if anyone who spends time with the children poses a risk to their safety or yours.

What to consider as a risk to safety to you or the children

A variety of behaviours can be considered abuse. If children see or hear someone else being treated badly, it can harm them. If you or the children have experienced or are at risk of experiencing any of these behaviours, it is considered a safety concern by the court:

Physical abuse

Examples of physical abuse includes action such as:

  • unching
  • choking
  • kicking
  • hitting with an object

Psychological abuse

Physical abuse is when someone is put in a situation that leads to:

  • anxiety
  • depression
  • post traumatic stress disorder

Emotional abuse

Emotional abuse could be spoken (verbal) or not involving words or speech (non-verbal). Examples include:

  • name calling
  • constant criticism
  • controlling behaviour
  • not letting them have an opinion

Sexual abuse

Examples of sexual abuse include:

  • being forced or pressured to have sex without consent
  • being threatened into an unwanted sexual activity
  • unwanted touching or groping

Financial abuse

Examples of financial abuse include:

  • not allowing a person to work
  • stopping someone saving their own money
  • withholding money or credit cards

Why do we need this information and what will we do with it?

The court needs to know if any of the other people in this application, or anyone connected to them who has contact with the children, poses a risk to the safety of the children.

If you can provide this information now, it will make it easier for the court and support services to make sure your case is handled correctly.

If you don't want to provide details of the abuse right now, you will be able to do so when you speak to the Children and Family Court Advisory Support Service (Cafcass).

The Children and Family Court Advisory and Support Service (Cafcass) in England, and Cafcass Cymru in Wales protects the interests of children involved in family court cases.

An advisor from Cafcass or Cafcass Cymru will look at your answers during safeguarding checks, and may need to ask you some more questions.

As part of this process they will get in touch with organisations such as the police and local authorities, for any information about you, the other people in the case and the children.

They will provide this information to the court before your first hearing. The judge will take their assessment into account when deciding what is best for the children.

The information you provide in this section will also be shared with the applicant, so they can respond to what you have said.
